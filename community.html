HOME SUGGESTED MEDIA

In regards to the medium of print, Bara makes up an insignificant amount of the male-male erotic manga genre. The popular male-male erotic manga characters are typically femme, young, submissive, and sometimes even resembling transgender individuals— known as a bishōnen (“pretty boy”) character. All genres of Bara emerged in the postwar era as a genre made by masculine gay men, for masculine gay men. Although the space in the world of comics is insignificant, the space for exploring Japanese identity, especially with its increase in access because of the internet, is. In an interview with the Huffington Post, Graham Kolbeins claims, “…[para] manga also owes a lot of Japan’s home-grown history of male-male sexual culture.”



Unlike other forms of bara art, manga sticks to its cultural routes. The appropriation seen in other genres of bara is hyper visible on the internet: When searched in google, the images that are shown are not necessarily Japanese — or traditionally considered bara — and with qualifiers in the search, like “anime,” “gif,” or “transparent,” these searches stray further from the definition of the word, often encompassing non-human, non-masculine men. In the gaming world, bara has a slight present in the dating sims world and various noncommercial flash games; however, many of these games, like Super Health Club are far removed from Japanese culture and feature a set predominately white characters interacting with one or two Japanese ones. This shift is not necessarily bad — the genre is still being used as a means of exploring identity —, but it often sets up stereotypical tropes (albeit racial) that the genre tried to remove itself from when it became a response to Yaoi. Like all cultures, new isn’t necessarily bad — but it is important to not forget its roots.
